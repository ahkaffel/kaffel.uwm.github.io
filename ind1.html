Professional Overview

I am an Applied Mathematician, Engineering Scientist, and Civil Engineer with extensive experience in mathematical modeling, numerical analysis, and computational engineering. My research activity is best summarized as the development of novel numerical methods—combined with classical tools of applied mathematics—to solve physical problems of current interest in fluid mechanics, porous media, biomedical imaging, and emerging data-driven scientific applications.

My academic and research appointments have included Brown University and the University of Maryland (Research Associate Scientist), and faculty positions at Prince Sultan University, Marquette University, and the University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee. Across these roles, I have led and contributed to interdisciplinary efforts in CFD, multiscale porous-media modeling, stability theory, inverse problems, and scientific computing.

Research at a Glance

Inverse Problems & Medical Imaging

Wavelet-based, meshless reconstruction algorithms for single-coil magnetic induction tomography (MIT), including regularized inversion for high-resolution conductivity imaging.

Thin Swelling Porous Media

Volume-averaged, Richards-type macroscopic models capturing unsaturated flow, inter-layer mass exchange, and deformation in multilayered thin porous structures.

Multiphase Flow & Stability

Hydrodynamic stability, interfacial modes, thin-film dynamics, and microchannel/microgap flows with rigorous spectral and eigenvalue-based analysis.

Signature Contributions

Wavelet Meshless MIT Imaging (Inverse Problem)

Developing a wavelet-based discretization of the MIT convolution integral to reduce reliance on precisely known target boundaries, enabling adaptive spatial resolution where it matters most. The approach integrates SVD-based inversion, regularized least squares, and uncertainty/error-aware reconstruction—supporting the long-term vision of low-cost, portable imaging for underserved communities.

  • Meshless wavelet discretization of convolution kernels and conductivity fields
  • SVD/regularization for underdetermined inversion with limited sensor data
  • Bridges applied linear algebra, optimization, parallel algorithms, and imaging physics

Industrial-Grade Porous Media Modeling (Thin Swelling Layers)

Developed rigorous volume-averaging upscaling frameworks that reduce micro-scale 3D transport to efficient quasi-2D models, including closure relations for inter-layer mass transfer and deformation. These models have direct relevance to absorbent materials and thin porous structures, with demonstrated industrial impact.

  • Richards-type unsaturated transport with deformation and closure modeling
  • Validated, cost-effective FEM simulation pipelines for design optimization
  • Extensions to fuel-cell water management and other thin porous systems

Multiphase Microgap Flow: Instabilities & Thin Films

Advanced theoretical and numerical frameworks for the stability of viscous two-layer flows in microchannels/microgaps, including modified Orr–Sommerfeld formulations solved using multi-domain Chebyshev collocation and QZ eigenvalue methods. This work clarifies competing shear and interfacial modes and supports predictive control of thin-film rupture.

  • Full eigenspectrum computation; long-/short-wave asymptotics matched to numerics
  • Interfacial wave selection via growth-rate maxima; nonlinear extensions via DNS
  • Documented rivulet formation as a dominant heat-transfer mechanism in annular regimes

Shear-Flow Stability in Complex Fluids

Ph.D. work on stability of parallel shear flows in viscoelastic fluids (upper-convected Maxwell model), emphasizing the interplay of inertia and elasticity in transition mechanisms. Implemented efficient spectral solvers and validated eigenvalue predictions against asymptotic limits.

  • Chebyshev spectral collocation for eigenvalue problems in stability theory
  • Parallel shear configurations: Poiseuille/Couette/shear layers/jets
  • Free-surface shear-flow short-wave modes and surface-localized instabilities

Teaching, Mentorship, and Inclusive Excellence

My teaching philosophy is anchored in innovation, interdisciplinarity, and inclusion. I design courses that build rigorous foundations while preparing students to apply mathematics in data-rich and computationally intensive environments. I integrate modern computational tools (e.g., MATLAB, Python, Mathematica, Fortran) and applied projects that connect differential equations, linear algebra, numerical methods, and modeling to real scientific and engineering challenges.

Across the U.S. and internationally, I have taught and mentored students from diverse backgrounds, emphasizing active learning, conceptual understanding, and supportive, high-expectation mentorship.
